# ASTM A193 and A194: The Material Backbone of Pressure Bolting

Our stud bolt and heavy hex nut articles both ended on the same point: **ASME standards give bolting its shape, ASTM gives it its strength.** This piece covers the two ASTM specifications that do most of that work in flange and pressure service — **A193** for the bolting, **A194** for the nuts — and how their grades pair up on a compliant purchase order.

## ASTM A193: High-Temperature and High-Pressure Bolting

A193 covers **alloy steel and stainless steel bolting material for high-temperature or high-pressure service** — studs, stud bolts, and bolts destined for flanges, valves, pressure vessels, and exchangers. Its grades fall into two families:

**Ferritic alloy steel (heat treated):**

- **B7** — quenched and tempered chromium-molybdenum steel; the default grade of the pressure industry, combining high strength with service capability into the low-400s °C range
- **B7M** — B7 with controlled (reduced) hardness and 100% hardness testing, specified for sour service environments under NACE MR0175 / ISO 15156
- **B16** — chromium-molybdenum-vanadium steel for elevated temperatures beyond B7's comfort zone

**Austenitic stainless:**

- **B8** (304) and **B8M** (316) — corrosion-resistant and suitable from cryogenic through high-temperature service
- **B8T** (321) and **B8C** (347) — stabilized grades for temperature cycling
- Each is available as **Class 1** (carbide solution treated) or **Class 2** (strain hardened for higher strength, with size-dependent properties)

One boundary worth knowing: A193 is the *high-temperature* specification. For low-temperature service with impact testing requirements, the sister spec **ASTM A320** (e.g., grade L7) takes over.

## ASTM A194: The Nuts That Match

A194 covers **carbon, alloy, and stainless steel nuts for high-pressure or high-temperature service** — overwhelmingly in the heavy hex pattern dimensioned per ASME B18.2.2. Its grade numbering deliberately mirrors the bolting it serves, which makes pairing straightforward:

| Bolting (A193) | Nut (A194) | Typical Service |
|---|---|---|
| B7 | **2H** | General refinery, pipeline, pressure service |
| B7M | **2HM** | Sour service (controlled hardness both sides) |
| B16 | **7** (or 4) | Elevated temperature |
| B8 Cl.1/Cl.2 | **8** | Stainless, corrosive / cryogenic |
| B8M Cl.1/Cl.2 | **8M** | 316 stainless, marine and chemical |

**The pairing is a requirement, not a convention.** The nut must develop the full proof load of the stud — a soft or mismatched nut caps the strength of the entire assembly. Sour-service joints are the strictest case: B7M studs demand 2HM nuts, because a standard 2H nut reintroduces the very hardness the specification exists to control.

## Traceability: Where These Specs Earn Their Keep

A193/A194 product lives or dies on documentation. Expect and specify:

- **Grade marking** on one stud end and on the nut face, plus the manufacturer's identifier
- **Heat number traceability** with mill test reports (EN 10204 3.1 certificates are the customary commercial ask)
- **Charpy or hardness testing** where the grade or service demands it (B7M's 100% hardness testing is a defining requirement, not an optional extra)

For buyers, the practical rule: if the certificate can't trace the lot to a heat and a grade marking, it isn't A193/A194 product regardless of what the box says.

## The Bottom Line

A193 and A194 are two halves of one system: matched bolting and nuts for the pressure industries, graded for temperature, corrosion, and sour-service regimes. Specify them as pairs — B7/2H, B7M/2HM, B8M/8M — insist on marking and heat traceability, and remember the boundaries: A320 below ambient extremes, B16 above B7's temperature ceiling. Get the pairing right and the paperwork right, and the metallurgy takes care of itself.
