# DIN 912 vs ISO 4762: A Professional Comparison

If you've ever received a drawing calling out **DIN 912** and a supplier quote offering **ISO 4762**, you've faced one of the most common cross-referencing questions in the fastener industry. The short answer: they describe the same product — the metric hexagon socket head cap screw — and are fully interchangeable. But the transition did change a few things around the edges, and those edges are exactly where sourcing surprises happen.

## Dimensionally Identical, Fully Interchangeable

Both standards specify the same dimensional requirements for socket head cap screws: cylindrical head roughly 1.5× the nominal diameter, head height approximately equal to the diameter, internal hexagon drive. **An M10 × 50 to either standard is physically and functionally the same screw.** DIN 912 has been formally withdrawn; the current designation is **ISO 4762** (DIN EN ISO 4762 in Germany), and it should be the reference on new drawings.

So where do the two differ? Not in the screw — in the catalogue around it.

## Change 1: The Size Range Shrank at Both Ends

| Aspect | DIN 912 | ISO 4762 |
|---|---|---|
| Nominal size range | M1.4 – M100 | M1.6 – M64 |
| Fine thread coverage | No dedicated standard | **ISO 12474** |
| Status | Withdrawn | Active |

DIN 912 covered an enormous span, from M1.4 miniatures up to M100. ISO 4762 trimmed the range to **M1.6 through M64**.

**What this means in practice:** M1.4 socket screws didn't vanish from the world, but they lost their mainstream standard home — expect longer lead times, fewer stocking distributors, and manufacturer-specific supply. At the top end, very large socket cap screws above M64 similarly become special-order territory referencing legacy or works standards. If your BOM lives at either extreme, flag it early in the RFQ rather than discovering it at quoting.

## Change 2: Fine Threads Got Their Own Standard

DIN 912 never had a dedicated fine-thread companion — fine-pitch socket screws were handled ad hoc. The ISO system fixed this by publishing **ISO 12474: hexagon socket head cap screws with metric fine pitch thread**. If your application needs fine pitch (thin-wall tapped sections, fine adjustment, vibration-sensitive joints), the correct modern callout is ISO 12474, not "ISO 4762 fine thread."

## Change 3: Preference Diameters Were Rationalised

ISO 4762 also pruned the catalogue, removing most second- and third-preference diameters that DIN 912 carried — with **M14 surviving as the notable exception** thanks to persistent market demand. The practical effect is a cleaner, more stockable size list: if a legacy drawing calls up an odd intermediate diameter, check current availability before assuming it's a shelf item.

## What to Do With a DIN 912 Callout

- **Buyers:** product certified to ISO 4762 satisfies a DIN 912 callout in virtually every commercial scenario; confirm documentation wording at order stage if the customer's QA is strict.
- **Engineers:** reference ISO 4762 (and ISO 12474 for fine pitch) on new drawings; keep an eye on sizes outside M1.6–M64 inherited from old designs.
- **Both:** treat odd diameters and range extremes as long-lead items, not catalogue stock.

## The Bottom Line

DIN 912 and ISO 4762 are the same screw with different paperwork — the real changes are in the range (M1.6–M64), the arrival of ISO 12474 for fine threads, and a rationalised diameter list. Specify ISO 4762, know where the catalogue edges moved, and this classic cross-reference will never slow a project down.
