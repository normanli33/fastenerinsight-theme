# ASTM F593: The Standard Behind Inch-Series Stainless Steel Fasteners

Our A193/A194 article covered stainless bolting for pressure service — but most stainless fasteners never see a flange. Architectural fixings, marine hardware, food processing equipment, and general machinery run on commercial stainless bolts, and in the inch world those are governed by **ASTM F593**. It's a compact specification, but its alloy groups, conditions, and marking suffixes confuse more RFQs than almost any other stainless standard.

## What ASTM F593 Covers

F593 is the specification for **stainless steel bolts, hex cap screws, and studs** in inch sizes, nominally **1/4" through 1-1/2" diameter**. Its companion for nuts is **ASTM F594** — the two are specified together, just as A193 pairs with A194.

The specification is organised along two axes: **what alloy** the fastener is made from, and **what condition** (processing route) it's supplied in. Both must appear in a complete callout.

## Axis One: Alloy Groups

F593 sorts stainless alloys into numbered groups. The two that dominate commercial supply:

- **Alloy Group 1** — the 18-8 family (304, 304L, 305 and close relatives): the general-purpose default
- **Alloy Group 2** — 316/316L: molybdenum-bearing, for marine, chloride, and chemical exposure

Further groups cover stabilized austenitics (321/347), ferritic and martensitic grades (430, 410, 416, 431), and precipitation-hardening alloys — available, but specialty territory compared with Groups 1 and 2.

## Axis Two: Conditions

Condition describes how the fastener was processed, which directly sets its strength:

| Condition | Meaning | Practical Note |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Machined from annealed stock | Lowest strength; softest, most corrosion-stable |
| **CW** | Headed/rolled and cold worked | The commercial standard; strength varies by diameter |
| **SH** | Strain hardened | Highest austenitic strength; limited sizes |

The subtlety buyers miss: **CW strength is size-dependent.** Smaller diameters pick up more cold work, so a 1/2" CW bolt carries a higher minimum tensile requirement than a 1-1/4" one made the same way. The specification splits CW into diameter bands for exactly this reason — never assume one tensile figure across a size range.

## The Marking Suffixes: F593C, F593G, and Friends

Heads are marked "F593" plus a letter that encodes the alloy-group-and-condition combination. The ones seen constantly in the market:

- **F593C / F593D** — Group 1 (304/18-8), cold worked, smaller / larger diameter bands
- **F593G / F593H** — Group 2 (316), cold worked, smaller / larger diameter bands

So when a customer sends a photo of a bolt head stamped **F593G**, they've told you the alloy family, the condition, and the diameter band in a single character. Quoting "304 where 316 was marked" is the fastest way to fail an incoming inspection.

## Metric Cross-Reference

F593 is the inch cousin of **ISO 3506** — the standard behind the familiar **A2-70 and A4-80** metric markings. The rough correspondence: Group 1 ≈ A2, Group 2 ≈ A4, and Condition CW in the smaller diameter band lands near property class 70. Useful for conversation; **not a substitution table** — thread forms, exact strength minima, and testing regimes differ, so mixed-standard supply needs explicit approval.

## The Bottom Line

An F593 callout is complete only when it names alloy group **and** condition — "F593 316 CW" says something; "stainless bolt" says nothing. Read the head-marking suffix, respect the diameter-dependent strength bands, pair with F594 nuts, and keep ISO 3506 as a reference point rather than a swap. That's the whole standard in four habits.
