# An Introduction to Heavy Hex Nuts: ASME B18.2.2 Explained

If the stud bolt is the workhorse of flange joints, the **heavy hex nut** is its inseparable partner — and the same nut shows up again on structural bolting, anchor bolts, and heavy machinery. The dimensional standard behind the inch-series product is **ASME B18.2.2**, and knowing what "heavy" actually means dimensionally will save you from one of the most common substitution errors in the trade.

## What ASME B18.2.2 Covers

ASME B18.2.2 — *Nuts for General Applications (Inch Series)* — is the umbrella dimensional standard for the familiar family of inch nuts: machine screw nuts, square nuts, hex nuts, hex jam nuts, slotted nuts, hex flange nuts, coupling nuts — and the **heavy hex pattern** in plain, jam, and slotted forms. Threads follow ASME B1.1 unified form, typically UNC up to 1" and 8UN above, mirroring the bolting they mate with.

## What Makes a Hex Nut "Heavy"

The heavy hex pattern is larger than a standard finished hex nut in both directions:

| Feature | Finished Hex Nut | Heavy Hex Nut |
|---|---|---|
| Width across flats | ≈ 1.5 × nominal diameter | ≈ 1.5 × diameter **+ 1/8"** |
| Thickness | Noticeably less than diameter | Approximately equal to nominal diameter |
| Example: 3/4" nut | 1-1/8" AF | 1-1/4" AF |

The extra width spreads bearing load over a larger flange face, and the extra thickness gives **more engaged threads** — which is precisely why heavy hex nuts are mandatory partners for high-strength bolting. A hardened bolt develops its full rated preload only when the nut has enough thread engagement to strip-proof the joint.

**The classic error:** substituting a standard hex nut on a high-strength stud or structural bolt because "the thread fits." It fits — and it under-delivers on proof load and wrench size, and it fails inspection. Nut pattern is a specification requirement, not a stylistic choice.

## Dimensions From ASME, Strength From ASTM

Like its stud bolt counterpart (ASME B18.31.2), B18.2.2 defines **geometry only**. Load capability comes from the ASTM material specification stamped on the nut face:

- **ASTM A194 2H** — quenched and tempered, the default partner for A193 B7 stud bolts in flange and pressure service
- **A194 8/8M** — stainless, mating B8/B8M studs
- **ASTM A563 grades A, C, and DH** — the general-purpose and structural range; DH pairs with F3125 Grade A325/A490 structural bolts
- **A194 grades 4 and 7** — low-temperature and alloy service alongside A320 bolting

A complete callout therefore reads: *heavy hex nut to ASME B18.2.2, 1"-8UN, ASTM A194 2H, hot-dip galvanized* — dimensional standard, thread, grade, finish.

## One Trap Worth Knowing: Galvanized Nuts Are Tapped Oversize

Hot-dip galvanized assemblies require the nut to be **tapped oversize** to accommodate the zinc layer on the mating thread. That makes galvanized nuts and bolts a **matched set**: order them together, from the same source where possible, and never mix a plain nut onto a galvanized stud or vice versa.

## Metric Context

Heavy hex is fundamentally an inch/ASME concept. The metric analogue is covered by **ASME B18.2.4.6M** (metric heavy hex nuts), while European structural practice reaches similar proportions through **DIN 6915 / EN 14399** nuts. None of these is a casual drop-in for the others — cross-specifying needs engineering sign-off.

## The Bottom Line

A heavy hex nut is not just a big hex nut — it's a defined pattern under ASME B18.2.2 with the width and thread engagement that high-strength bolting depends on. Specify the pattern, the ASTM grade, and the finish together, keep galvanized components as matched sets, and the nut will never be the weak point in the joint.
